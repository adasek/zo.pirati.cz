Lukáš Doležal

Digital Democracy

The Internet marked a turning point in how we receive information and communicate. The establishment of the Pirate Party is closely linked to the rise of the Internet, and political participation of the general public has always been an important point on the Pirate agenda. The increased availability of the Internet, urbanization, globalization, and other phenomena of the last two decades have made it much more necessary to associate and make political decisions online.
A fundamental prerequisite for the development of digital democracy is that society becomes more democratized.

But what is the reality today? Are we explaining the benefits of citizens’ digital participation on public affairs enough? Do people understand the possibilities offered by the instruments of digital democracy?
Where are we now? Why can we still not vote online in the Czech Republic, when this option has existed in Estonia since 2001? What are the risks and opportunities of online elections? How can we make decisions about the place where we live over the Internet?

We are looking for contributions to the Digital Democracy program section dealing with e-voting, e-democracy, transparency of online voting, direct democracy, and participatory budgeting. We are also looking for examples of best practices of public engagement through the Internet and international examples of online public opinion polls or hardware and software solutions for electronic voting (legislative barriers, end-to-end verification, the role of open-source, voter identification and authentication).
